# Leadership at YourGP

**Competency · Coachability · Clarity · Courage · Character**

Everyone at YourGP can be a leader. These standards apply regardless of your role or title.

---

## Purpose

At YourGP, how you lead matters as much as what you achieve. This document defines the leadership standard we hold ourselves to -- built on five qualities we call the **5 C's**: Competency, Coachability, Clarity, Courage, and Character.

It draws heavily on Andy Stanley's *Next Generation Leader*, alongside the thinking of Brené Brown, Simon Sinek, Patrick Lencioni, and others.

### How to Use This Document

- **When you join**: Read the 5 C's summary and scan the 15 standards
- **Before your review**: Work through the Leadership Self-Assessment honestly
- **When something feels hard**: Find the standard that fits and sit with it for a bit
- **In team huddles**: Use the Daily Standards Summary at the end

**Nobody is expected to be all of this perfectly.** What we expect is honesty about where we are, a genuine commitment to getting better, and the willingness to have real conversations about both.

## The 5 C's — At a Glance

### Competency
**In One Sentence**: Play to your strengths, delegate your weaknesses, develop others.

### Coachability
**In One Sentence**: The best leaders never stop learning -- and they let others help them see what they cannot.

### Clarity
**In One Sentence**: Be clear even when you are not certain -- clarity is perceived as leadership.

### Courage
**In One Sentence**: Move despite fear, decide despite uncertainty, challenge despite comfort.

### Character
**In One Sentence**: The product of the other four -- who you become when competency, coachability, clarity, and courage are lived consistently.

## Leadership Standards

### Competency

It is both natural and necessary for leaders to try to prove themselves by doing everything themselves. But the two best-kept secrets of leadership are: 
- The less you do, the more you accomplish
- The less you do, the more you enable others to accomplish

#### 1. Own the whole job -- and know where you shine

Your particular strengths are where you add the most value, but you have to own the whole job. You are responsible for everything in your role, not just the parts you enjoy or the parts you are naturally good at.

When you understand what you do better than anyone else around you, you can lean into that strength to lift the whole team. Delegate when you can. Ask for help when you need it. But never confuse "this isn't my strength" with "this isn't my job."

> "The key to high performance is to find your areas of greatest strength and invest your time there." -- Marcus Buckingham

#### 2. Know the difference between authority and competency

Every leader has authority over areas in which they have little or no competence. When you exert authority in an area where you lack competence, you derail projects and demotivate people.

Work within your delegation. Seek permission rather than forgiveness when operating at its edge. Build relationships with your peers based on mutual respect. Collaboration is not a soft skill -- it is how good organisations work.

> "None of us is as smart as all of us." -- Ken Blanchard

#### 3. Grow the people around you

Measure your success not just by what you personally deliver, but by how much better the people around you are becoming. Great leaders work through other leaders who work through others.

A team that depends entirely on you is not a success -- it is a risk.

> "Before you are a leader, success is all about growing yourself. When you become a leader, success is all about growing others." -- Jack Welch

### Coachability

Great leaders are great learners. Even Andre Agassi, at the peak of his career, credited his coach as immeasurably important. Age and experience do not diminish the need for a coach.

#### 4. Stay coachable -- always

Seek feedback and act on it. Stay open to being wrong, even when you are the most experienced person in the room. The moment you stop learning is the moment you start limiting the people around you.

Do not surround yourself only with people who agree with you.

> "It is not the accomplishments of the coach that make them a valuable ally. It is what they see." -- Andy Stanley

#### 5. Seek feedback like your leadership depends on it

It does. Actively seek feedback from the people you lead, the people who lead you, and the people beside you.

Admitting your mistakes openly does not diminish your strength. It shows you have the courage to be honest. When you acknowledge what you do not know, the people around you will feel safe to do the same.

> "I believe providing feedback is the most cost-effective strategy for improving performance and instilling satisfaction." -- Ken Blanchard

#### 6. Measure yourself against your potential, not your peers

Stop comparing yourself to those around you. Complacency creeps in when you look sideways instead of upward.

> "Anyone who stops learning is old, whether at twenty or eighty. Anyone who keeps learning stays young." -- Henry Ford

### Clarity

Be clear even when you are not certain. The only thing we can be certain about is the past. Clarity of vision is more important than certainty of outcome.

#### 7. Communicate with clarity and say the hard things early

Speak simply and directly. Help people understand not just what to do but why it matters. Have the difficult conversation before it becomes a crisis.

You do not need certainty to be clear. Acknowledge what you know, what you do not know, and what you plan to do about it.

> "Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind." -- Brené Brown

#### 8. Look up and look ahead

Your job is not just to manage today -- it is to prepare for tomorrow. Understand where YourGP is going and help your team see it too.

Pencil in your plans. Etch the vision in stone.

> "Leadership is the capacity to translate vision into reality." -- Warren Bennis

#### 9. Face current reality

Before you can lead people somewhere better, you need to be honest about where you are now.

**Thou shalt not:**
1. Pretend
2. Turn a blind eye
3. Exaggerate
4. Shoot the bearer of bad news
5. Hide behind the numbers
6. Ignore constructive criticism
7. Isolate yourself

### Courage

Leaders are not always the first to see the need for change, but they are the first to act. Courage establishes leadership.

#### 10. Make the call

Gather the information you need, think it through, and then decide. Do not wait for certainty that will never come.

**Careful vs Fearful:**
- Careful: Cerebral, calculates risk, aims for success, concerned about progress
- Fearful: Emotional, avoids risk, aims to avoid failure, concerned about protection

Courage is not the absence of fear. It is the ability to move despite it.

> "You can't lead without risk. You can't take risk without courage." -- Andy Stanley

#### 11. Stay steady when things are hard

When change comes -- and it will -- be the person your team looks to for calm and direction. Your steadiness under pressure matters more than your performance in calm waters.

> "Adversity is an opportunity for heroism." -- Coach K

#### 12. Never accept that the current way is the only way

Look for friction in how things work and ask whether it has to be there. Try new approaches. You do not have to have all the answers -- but you do have to stay curious.

> "There is no innovation and creativity without failure. Period." -- Brené Brown

### Character

Character is not essential to leadership. But character is what makes you a leader worth following.

Every leader wears two badges: one visible (your position and title) and one invisible (your moral authority).

#### 13. Lead with honesty and integrity

Say what you mean. Do what you say. Act the same whether or not anyone is watching.

Moral authority is the credibility people see between what you claim to be and what you really are. It takes years to build and one moment to damage.

> "Your talent and giftedness as a leader have the potential to take you farther than your character can sustain you. That ought to scare you." -- Andy Stanley

#### 14. Make sure everyone belongs

Value different perspectives and do not confuse confidence with correctness. Create an environment where people feel safe to speak up, to disagree, and to contribute fully.

Belonging is not a policy. It is a daily practice. How you treat the newest, quietest, or most different person in the room defines your leadership.

> "Every person on your team is important, and every role matters." -- Coach K

#### 15. Be someone people can rely on

Do what you said you would do, by when you said you would do it. Be consistent in how you show up.

There is no cramming for a test of character. It always comes as a pop quiz.

> "There is no cramming for a test of character. It always comes as a pop quiz." -- Andy Stanley
